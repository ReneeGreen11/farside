## Stomach Acid and Its Role in Digestion (and Beyond)

The most obvious role of stomach acid is to break down food, which is essential for proper digestion. Without enough acidity, even a very healthy diet can make you feel wretched. You may wonder why. Without sufficient stomach acid, that nutritious food simply sits in your stomach and intestines and begins to rot. It then feeds bacterial and fungal communities that are not supposed to be there. How do they get there?

This brings us to the second major role of stomach acidity—one that receives far less attention: it acts as a powerful filter, preventing harmful bacteria from entering the small intestine.

A 2015 study1 published in PLOS ONE hypothesized that scavenger animals would have the highest levels of stomach acidity as a defense mechanism to protect against harmful bacteria from their food.

The authors noted:
“Given its primary role in digestion, stomach pH has been measured in far fewer taxa than might be expected. For instance, to the best of our knowledge, no data on stomach pH exist for any hominoid other than humans, and surprisingly few data exist for primates more generally. Similarly, while it is widely held that hyenas have ‘very acidic’ stomachs, consultation of experts in hyenas and their diets revealed that no one was aware of data that actually directly tested this assertion.”

There appears to be surprisingly little scientific interest in this topic. This lack of attention may help explain why so many people suffer from poor digestion today—and why certain common medical practices, such as gastric bypass surgery or the overuse of proton pump inhibitors (PPIs) and antacids, can cause unintended harm. The medical community as a whole often underestimates the serious consequences of a non-acidic stomach environment.

One finding that surprised the researchers was that a healthy human stomach is highly acidic—comparable to that of carrion-feeding animals (scavengers that eat rotting carcasses rather than freshly killed prey). They offered two possible explanations:

One is that carrion feeding may have played a more important role in human (and more broadly hominin) evolution than is currently recognized. Alternatively, given the high number of fecal-oral pathogens that infect and kill humans, natural selection may have favored high stomach acidity—independent of diet—because of its critical role in preventing pathogen invasion.

The study also observed that the people most vulnerable to bacterial infections originating in the stomach and small intestine (fungal overgrowth is likely a similar risk, though not explicitly mentioned) include infants, the elderly, and individuals using PPIs or who have undergone bariatric surgery.

Something worth considering: many people online report significant improvements in digestion after supplementing with hydrochloric acid (HCl, often as betaine HCl).
